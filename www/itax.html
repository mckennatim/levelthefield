Income data - IRS SOI,

Aka Statistic of Income, is put out by the IRS. There is data on the top 400, on each of the quintiles (each quintile is 20% of the population). There in some data on the top 5% and some on the top 1% and .5%. Other data on slices of the richest of our population comes from the work of Prof. Emmanuel Saez. David Cay Johnston references that work in an article for Tax Analysts, published on tax.com. Basically you see the average incomes of each group. Multiply those by the number of people and you get a sense of where the money is.

Ord. Income Brackets.

The current Obama / Bush system has a set of income brackets and the rates for each bracket. Rich people pay the same rate on the first x dollars as everyone else. The top rate kicks in very early some that even families making less thatn $200,000 are paying at the same ordinary income rate as the those with millions of dollars in income.

Capital Gains are different.

There are two basic classes of income.

C.Gains/Q.Dividends are taxed at a lower rate

Ordinary income is taxed at the wage rates ie. 15%,25%,30%,33%&35%. Capital gains and Qualified Dividends is taxed at 15%. On a phone this graph (below) is unreadable; the white area in the upper right is capital gains, the lower dark band is dividends. For the rich, most of their income is 'unearned'; coming from investments that pay dividends and have capital gains. The argument is that low capital gains rates is good for Americans; there is even a special 0% rate if you make less than $30,000. Trouble is those who make $30,000, on average, have about 2-4% 'unearned' income. Even if your income is $100,000, you are likely to have less that 10% capital gains/dividend income. For the rich the numbers are reversed. ) Once you cross the million dollar mark (about the middle of the chart), then more than %50 of your income is likely to be from capital gains and (qualified (US corp)) dividends.

for creating better income tax system

To create a plan you need to set the level of deductions, decide on how treat Capital Gains / Dividends and determine the tax rates and the income brackets to which they apply. You can do these in any order; there are tabs on the next page to move you back and forth. The goal is to match the target while creating a better system. You can tell you have reached your goal when the text turns from blue to yellow. What follows here is help for each task.

Deductions

Deductions represent the amount of income is not taxed. They include things like mortgage interest and charitable contributions. For deductions you can leave things alone using the current typical deductions from the IRS data, make a radical change and set deductions at the same amount for people of every income, or create a combination of the two.

The case for equal deductions for every income level centers around the idea that every American starts paying taxes at the point where their basic needs for food clothing and shelter have been met.

In combining the two systems consider the 'totals' section at the bottom of the Deduction Model box. It might be a good idea to create a system that has the same overall total of income deductions. Or not. If you decide to 'mix' the current system and the fixed system, you can use the slider to choose the proportions and can see the implications in the table below the slider.

Capital Gains

review the argument:

Argument for low/no capital gains rates
"To many conservatives, it is a matter of faith that the tax rate on capital gains should be lower than ordinary rates, or even zero. They say that lower tax rates encourage risk-taking and entrepreneurship and offset the double taxation of corporate profits." Recent tax law lowers or even eliminates the tax for lower income taxpayers.
Argument to treat them as ordinary income,
In spite of carefully orchestrated advertising to the contrary, the current system predominantly benefits the wealthy. "In 2010, the average person with income under $200,000 had a capital loss, not a gain. Only the top 10 percent had gains on average, and people earning over $1 million had $258 billion of the $261 billion of net gains reported on tax returns. That is 97% of the gains went to people earning over $1 million. In 2008, Janet Novack reports, the richest 400 taxpayers - income over $110 million - earned 13.1% of capital gains." (Len Burman, professor at Syracuse, in Forbes from Mitt Romney's Teachable Moment on Capital Gains

It's a ginormous loophole

"Taxing capital gains at much lower rates than other income creates a ginormous loophole that leads to a tremendous amount of inefficient tax shelter activity. Virtually every individual income tax shelter is devoted to converting fully taxed income into capital gains. If you can transform $10 million of wages into gains, you can save over $2 million. With that kind of payoff, there is a whole industry devoted to inventing schemes to generate current deductions to shelter the wages and ultimately recoup it years later as lightly taxed gains. These shelter schemes entail inefficiency for at least two reasons. First, the investments that work in tax shelter plans are often very inefficient—the kinds of projects that would never attract capital in a rational world. Second, the people who put these schemes together are very intelligent and absent the capital gains windfall might actually do socially productive work like, say, producing American products that people around the world would like to buy."

(Len Burman, professor at Syracuse, in Forbes from Mitt Romney's Teachable Moment on Capital Gains)
